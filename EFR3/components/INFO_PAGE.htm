Background

Geert Hofstede, Gert Jan Hofstede, Michael Minkov and others offer 6 “dimensions of culture”. These describe basic issues for society to organize itself (based on a comprehensive study of how values in the workplace are influenced by culture; collected within IBM, 1967 to 1973). They do not exist, they are the product of our imagination. Culture is defined here “as the collective mental programming of the human mind which distinguishes one group of people from another”. As such, culture influences patterns of thinking which again is reflected in the meaning that people attach to various aspects of life and which become crystallized in the institutions of a society or as habitus in the various social milieus. Obviously, not everyone of us thinks the same, but most of us are strongly influenced by social control. So any statement on the culture of a nation is a generalization. The cultural dimensions represent independent preferences, which distinguish countries from one another and become only meaningful by comparison. Today Hofstede’s work is continued in the GLOBE project which defines 9 dimensions and focusses more on societal culture in institutions and enterprises.

Hofstede Dimensions

The dimensions can be summarized as follows:

1) Power Distance: Extent to which the less powerful (like the family) accept and expect that power is distributed unequally; society’s handling of inequalities among people

2) Individualism: Extent to which people feel independent vs feeling as part of a larger whole; degree of interdependence a society maintains among its members; people’s self-image

3) Masculinity: Extent to which the use of force is endorsed socially / about expected emotional gender roles; preference in society for: driven by competition, achievement and success vs caring for others and quality of life; what motivates people (sociocultural)

4) Uncertainty Avoidance: Deals with people’s or a society’s tolerance for uncertainty and ambiguity (anxiety and distrust in the face of the unknown or wish for fixed habits and rituals and to know the truth)

5) Long-term Orientation: Deals with change: preparing for the future is always needed (long-term) vs the past provides a moral compass (short-term); orientation of people or the society

6) Indulgence: In an indulgent culture it is good to be free, in a restrained culture the feeling is that life is hard and duty; based on the way people were raised and socialized as small children

Idea for a Map App

Today, people do not only like to travel for experiencing other countries, they also consider spending time abroad for studying or working reasons. Questions arise if one would feel comfortable or could even feel at home in these countries. Instead of checking each country individually, the idea is to allow for a worldwide comparison based on the Hofstede dimensions of culture.

Aims of the ‘Ideal Home Finder’ App

o   By providing a world map, a global view is offered to the user presenting also those parts of our world, which might currently not be in the mind of the user.

o   By considering user input, the world map is personalized and therefore more attractive as it talks / responds to the user.

o   By including interactivity, the user can explore the map, which prompts visual thinking about what is reflected on the map.

o   By offering a comment field, the user can share his/her discoveries, opinions or feelings with others.

o   By making other users’ world maps and comments available, the map app does not remain a personal effort but opens up to others and thus widens reflections.

Realization of the ‘Ideal Home Finder’ App

The app consists of five components: user input via rating, visualization on a world map, interactive exploration tools, a possibility for comments, and data storage. It starts by asking the user one question per dimension, which results in a rating of what he or she considers to reflect her ideas of feeling comfortable in a society. By requesting also an importance ranking, the app derives a value per country by means of calculating the sum of the differences between the values specified by the users and those gathered by Hofstede and others. These values are visualized on a world map using five colour shades and can be considered as indicator for the likelihood of feeling at home in a certain country. Here only countries with an area larger than 5000 km² are considered, i.e. those visible on the world map. Interactivity offers to explore which of the countries based on own rating would fit best to own cultural expectations and why. A comment field allows for statements as the user might discover that the resulting world map is not matching with own experiences or expectations and likes to reflect on it. Only if consent was given, the user’s input is anonymously stored and can thus also be viewed by other.

Technically, for the exhibition a robust offline version of a browser-based application employing mainly HTML5 and JavaScript was favoured. The app is database-driven (using SQLite) for providing the Hofstede dimension values plus for storing user input und results.
